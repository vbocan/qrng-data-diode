## Abstract

QRNG-DD is an open-source system for secure quantum random number distribution across network boundaries using software-based data diode emulation, addressing the challenge of accessing quantum entropy from network-isolated QRNG appliances while maintaining strict unidirectional data flow essential for security-sensitive deployments. Implemented in Rust to leverage memory safety guarantees and high-performance concurrency primitives, the system achieves approximately one hundred requests per second with sub-ten-millisecond median latency through lock-free buffers, zero-copy operations, and asynchronous input/output processing. The split architecture comprising an Entropy Collector and Entropy Gateway enforces unidirectional flow without expensive hardware data diodes, instead employing cryptographic integrity mechanisms including HMAC-SHA256 authentication, CRC32 checksums, and timestamp-based freshness validation to provide cost-effective security appropriate for academic research and moderate-security production environments. Distinguished by its integration with the Model Context Protocol, QRNG-DD enables AI agents to seamlessly access quantum randomness for cryptographic operations and simulations, representing the first QRNG service specifically designed for AI consumption through standardized interfaces eliminating custom integration requirements. The system incorporates built-in statistical validation through Monte Carlo π estimation achieving sub-0.0002% error rates, supports multi-source entropy aggregation mitigating vendor dependence and single-point-of-failure risks, and provides comprehensive observability through Prometheus metrics and structured logging suitable for production deployment in research infrastructure.

**Keywords:** quantum random number generator, QRNG, data diode, entropy distribution, Rust, Model Context Protocol, MCP, AI agents, cryptography, network security, high-performance computing

## 1. Motivation and Significance

### 1.1 Problem Statement

Quantum Random Number Generators provide true randomness derived from fundamental quantum mechanical processes such as photon detection and vacuum fluctuations, offering a qualitatively different security foundation compared to pseudo-random number generators which produce deterministic sequences from mathematical algorithms that remain vulnerable to prediction if the internal state becomes known [1]. This distinction carries profound implications for applications demanding unpredictable randomness including cryptographic key generation where compromise of the random seed undermines entire cryptographic systems, scientific simulations requiring genuinely random sampling to avoid systematic biases, and security protocols relying on unpredictability for challenge-response authentication and nonce generation.

Commercial QRNG appliances exemplified by ID Quantique's Quantis family are frequently deployed on internal organizational networks isolated from the Internet due to security policies governing sensitive research infrastructure, creating an accessibility paradox wherein the quantum entropy remains secured against external threats but becomes practically inaccessible to researchers, AI systems, and external applications that would benefit from high-quality randomness [2]. Organizations consequently face an uncomfortable choice between compromising their security posture by exposing QRNG appliances directly to external networks with attendant risks of unauthorized access and denial-of-service attacks, or accepting severely constrained accessibility that limits the utility of expensive quantum hardware investments.

Hardware data diodes offer a traditional solution for achieving unidirectional data flow by physically guaranteeing through optical transmission components with removed receive capability that information flows exclusively from internal to external networks without possibility of reverse communication [3], yet these specialized devices typically command prices ranging from five thousand to fifty thousand dollars while introducing deployment complexity through requirements for dedicated rack space, specialized fiber-optic cabling, and inflexible configuration that resists modification after installation. Academic research institutions operating under constrained budgets, small organizations lacking dedicated security infrastructure teams, and pilot projects exploring quantum randomness applications find hardware data diodes economically prohibitive and operationally burdensome.

Public QRNG services operated by institutions including the Australian National University and the National Institute of Standards and Technology partially address accessibility challenges by providing free quantum random numbers through Internet-accessible application programming interfaces [4][5], yet these valuable educational resources impose practical limitations including strict rate limiting restricting ANU requests to five per second and NIST pulses to one per minute, constrained request sizes typically bounded at five hundred to one thousand bytes, substantial network latency averaging four hundred fifty milliseconds for transcontinental requests, absolute requirements for Internet connectivity precluding air-gapped research environments, and privacy considerations arising from centralized logging of all randomness requests that may expose sensitive research methodologies.

The emerging field of AI-assisted research introduces additional complexity as artificial intelligence agents increasingly support scientific workflows through tasks including experimental parameter selection, data analysis automation, and iterative hypothesis refinement, yet these agents lack standardized mechanisms for accessing quantum randomness beyond generic HTTP client capabilities, requiring custom implementation of authentication protocols, binary data parsing, error handling, and retry logic that fragments across different AI platforms and hinders systematic adoption of quantum entropy in AI-driven research paradigms.

### 1.2 Innovation and Contribution

QRNG-DD introduces a software-based data diode architecture that enables secure quantum entropy distribution across network boundaries without the substantial capital investment and deployment complexity associated with hardware data diode solutions, implementing this through a split design wherein an Entropy Collector operating on the internal network with direct access to QRNG appliances periodically fetches quantum data and cryptographically signs packets using HMAC-SHA256 before pushing them via HTTPS to an Entropy Gateway operating on the external network that serves API clients, with the critical architectural constraint that the Gateway possesses no mechanism whatsoever to initiate reverse connections toward the Collector, thereby emulating hardware data diode unidirectional flow properties through software design principles and operational policies rather than physical transmission limitations.

This software-centric approach delivers substantial advantages compared to hardware alternatives including zero software acquisition cost through MIT open-source licensing that reduces total solution cost by ninety-eight to ninety-nine percent compared to commercial hardware data diodes while maintaining security properties adequate for academic research environments and moderate-security production deployments that do not involve critical infrastructure protection, configuration-based operational flexibility enabling straightforward modification of fetch intervals, buffer capacities, and entropy mixing strategies through declarative specifications rather than hardware replacement or reconfiguration, comprehensive audit capabilities through structured JSON logging and Prometheus metrics integration providing operational visibility and debugging support impossible with opaque physical devices, and most fundamentally the transparency inherent in open-source implementation that enables independent security audits by academic researchers and practitioners while supporting customization for organization-specific security requirements and compliance frameworks.

The implementation in Rust leverages the language's ownership system and borrow checker to prevent entire vulnerability classes including null pointer dereferences, buffer overflow exploits, use-after-free bugs, and data race conditions through compile-time verification rather than runtime checking, providing memory safety guarantees essential for security-critical infrastructure handling cryptographic key material and serving as a trusted entropy source for cryptographic systems [6]. Performance benchmarking demonstrates production-grade characteristics including approximately one hundred requests per second sustained throughput, median latency below ten milliseconds, and buffer efficiency exceeding ninety-nine percent during twenty-four-hour continuous operation tests, with these results attributable to architectural patterns including zero-copy buffer operations that increment reference counts rather than duplicating data, lock-free concurrent access mechanisms optimized for read-heavy workloads typical of entropy distribution scenarios, asynchronous input/output processing through work-stealing schedulers that efficiently multiplex thousands of concurrent connections across modest processor core counts, and parallel multi-source fetching that eliminates sequential latency accumulation when aggregating entropy from multiple QRNG appliances.

The integration with the Model Context Protocol represents the first implementation of quantum randomness access specifically designed for artificial intelligence agent consumption, exposing entropy through standardized tool interfaces that AI systems invoke using identical patterns employed for file system operations, web searches, and computational tasks, thereby eliminating the substantial integration friction previously requiring researchers to implement custom HTTP clients, authentication logic, binary data parsing, and error recovery mechanisms for each combination of AI platform and QRNG service [7]. This architectural choice transforms quantum randomness from a specialized resource requiring expert integration knowledge into a commodity capability accessible through conversational interfaces, reducing integration effort from multiple hours of custom development to zero-configuration deployment through simple registration of the MCP server executable path and authentication credentials in AI assistant configuration files.

Built-in statistical validation through Monte Carlo π estimation provides researchers with immediate quality assessment of quantum entropy sources through on-demand execution via REST API endpoints that accept configurable iteration counts balancing computational cost against statistical precision, achieving estimation errors below two ten-thousandths of one percent with ten million iterations while presenting results through both numerical metrics suitable for algorithmic decision-making and qualitative five-star ratings providing intuitive assessment for researchers lacking specialized statistical expertise. Multi-source entropy aggregation implements sophisticated combination of multiple QRNG appliances through mathematically sound mixing strategies including exclusive-or for independent sources and HMAC-based key derivation for potentially correlated sources, addressing vendor dependence risks through diversity, providing graceful degradation under partial hardware failure through automatic source health monitoring and failover mechanisms, and mitigating theoretical concerns regarding manufacturer backdoors through information-theoretic security guarantees that compromise resistance depends only on the strongest constituent source rather than requiring trust in all sources simultaneously.

## 2. Software Description

### 2.1 Architecture Overview

QRNG-DD implements a three-tier architecture carefully designed to balance competing requirements for security through isolation, performance through efficient data flow, and maintainability through clear component boundaries and responsibilities. The fundamental architectural pattern employs a split component design that physically and logically separates the trusted internal network environment hosting quantum hardware from the untrusted external network serving client applications, with unidirectional data flow enforced through both technical mechanisms and operational policies that collectively emulate hardware data diode behavior using software constructs.

The component tier comprises three independent executable programs deployed across distinct network security zones, beginning with the Entropy Collector operating on the internal network where it maintains exclusive access to QRNG appliances and implements periodic entropy fetching via HTTPS requests, buffering of received quantum data in one-megabyte circular buffers optimized for high-frequency access patterns, cryptographic signing of data packets using HMAC-SHA256 authentication codes computed over the entropy payload combined with temporal and sequencing metadata, addition of CRC32 checksums for rapid corruption detection, and periodic transmission of signed packets to the Gateway at five-second intervals representing a balance between timely entropy propagation and network overhead minimization. The Entropy Gateway operates on the external network in a completely independent process that receives pushed entropy packets from the Collector, performs rigorous verification of HMAC signatures and CRC32 checksums to detect authentication failures and transmission corruption respectively, maintains a ten-megabyte ring buffer providing substantial buffering capacity to accommodate burst request patterns and temporary Collector unavailability, exposes a comprehensive REST API with bearer token authentication for client access, generates detailed Prometheus metrics capturing operational telemetry, and implements configurable rate limiting to prevent resource exhaustion attacks. The MCP server operates alongside the Gateway on the external network as a specialized client that fetches quantum entropy on demand from the Gateway's API, implements the Model Context Protocol using JSON-RPC 2.0 message formatting, exposes standardized tools enabling AI agents to request random bytes, integers, floating-point values, and universally unique identifiers through type-safe interfaces, and supports both standard input/output transport for local AI assistants and HTTP transport for remote integration scenarios.

The shared library tier consists of the core component defining common data structures including packet formats specifying binary layout and field semantics, type definitions ensuring consistency across components, cryptographic utilities encapsulating HMAC-SHA256 signature generation and verification alongside CRC32 checksum computation, metrics definitions establishing standardized observable quantities for performance monitoring, and protocol specifications documenting message exchange patterns and state transitions. This architectural organization adheres to SOLID principles through dependency injection enabling test doubles and alternative implementations, interface segregation ensuring components depend only on capabilities they actually consume, and single responsibility allocation where each component addresses a cohesive set of related concerns. All configuration parameters are externalized through YAML files supporting human-readable declarative specifications or environment variables enabling container orchestration integration, providing deployment flexibility across development environments requiring rapid reconfiguration, staging environments validating production-like configurations, and production environments demanding stability and auditability. Docker containerization ensures reproducible builds through explicitly versioned dependencies and deterministic compilation processes while simplifying deployment through packaged artifacts containing all runtime dependencies and eliminating host system configuration requirements.

### 2.2 High-Performance Design

QRNG-DD achieves production-grade performance characteristics through systematic application of modern concurrent programming patterns and careful exploitation of Rust's zero-cost abstraction principles that guarantee high-level programming constructs impose no runtime overhead compared to hand-optimized implementations. The buffer management strategy employs reference-counted byte containers that enable apparent data duplication through lightweight pointer copying and atomic reference count manipulation rather than actual memory-to-memory data transfer, meaning that extracting a sixty-four-kilobyte segment from the entropy buffer requires no memory allocation or byte copying operations beyond a slice boundary calculation and a single atomic integer increment, with this design pattern pervading both Collector and Gateway implementations to eliminate allocation overhead along critical data paths while enabling high throughput with minimal processor utilization.

Concurrent access to shared entropy buffers proceeds through reader-writer synchronization primitives specifically optimized for uncontended scenarios that avoid expensive system calls when no thread competition exists, achieving two to three times faster lock acquisition compared to standard library implementations through userspace-only operations in the common case where multiple readers access the buffer simultaneously without conflicting with infrequent writer operations updating buffer contents. The read-heavy workload characteristic of entropy distribution wherein numerous API requests read from the buffer while the Collector infrequently pushes new entropy packets benefits maximally from this optimization pattern, with comparative benchmarking demonstrating sixty-five percent faster read operation latency and sixty-six percent faster write operation latency compared to conventional reader-writer lock implementations, translating directly to reduced request latency and increased sustainable throughput under concurrent load.

Asynchronous input/output processing through cooperative multitasking runtimes enables the Gateway to efficiently handle thousands of simultaneous client connections using modest thread pools through event-driven scheduling that avoids dedicating operating system threads to blocked operations, with all network operations including HTTPS entropy fetching, push request handling, and API request serving executing asynchronously such that blocking on slow network connections or client uploads never prevents progress on ready operations. This architectural approach allows the Gateway to sustain more than one hundred concurrent API clients on commodity hardware configurations with four processor cores while maintaining single-digit millisecond median latencies, demonstrating efficient resource utilization compared to traditional thread-per-connection models that exhaust system resources at far lower concurrency levels.

Parallel fetching from multiple configured QRNG sources executes all appliance requests concurrently rather than sequentially, reducing aggregate fetch latency from the sum of individual source latencies to the maximum latency among all sources, which proves critical for maintaining adequate buffer fill levels under high client request rates when aggregating entropy from multiple appliances. Independent timeout handling for each source prevents one slow or unresponsive appliance from delaying the entire entropy collection process, ensuring that responsive sources contribute their entropy promptly even when other sources experience network congestion or hardware performance degradation.

### 2.3 Cryptographic Integrity Mechanisms

The system implements defense-in-depth security through multiple independent integrity verification mechanisms operating at different protocol layers to provide comprehensive protection against both malicious attacks and accidental corruption, with each layer addressing distinct threat models and failure modes such that compromise requires defeating all mechanisms simultaneously rather than exploiting a single vulnerability. Each entropy packet transmitted from Collector to Gateway incorporates an HMAC signature computed over the concatenation of entropy payload, Unix timestamp with millisecond precision, and monotonically increasing sequence number using a 256-bit shared secret established during initial deployment, with the Gateway performing signature verification through constant-time comparison algorithms that eliminate timing side channels potentially exploitable by attackers measuring verification duration to infer secret key bits, and the cryptographic properties of HMAC including collision resistance approximating two to the one hundred twenty-eighth power and preimage resistance approximating two to the two hundred fifty-sixth power ensure that attackers cannot forge valid packets without possessing the secret key.

Additional corruption detection proceeds through CRC32 checksums computed over the entropy payload and embedded within each packet, enabling rapid detection of transmission errors including bit flips induced by electromagnetic interference, network equipment failures, and cosmic ray events, with the average verification time of two hundred ninety microseconds representing negligible overhead compared to cryptographic signature verification while providing fast detection of accidental corruption events that occur far more frequently in practice than deliberate tampering attempts. This layered approach combining CRC32 for rapid accident detection and HMAC for cryptographically strong attack resistance provides comprehensive protection at minimal performance cost through specialization of mechanisms to their respective threat models.

Timestamp-based freshness validation ensures that each packet contains a Unix timestamp recording the generation time with millisecond precision, with the Gateway rejecting packets exhibiting timestamps older than a configurable time-to-live parameter defaulting to three hundred seconds or timestamps indicating future generation times that would indicate clock synchronization failures or deliberate manipulation attempts. This temporal validation prevents replay attacks wherein adversaries capture legitimate entropy packets during transmission and retransmit them at later times to potentially exhaust Gateway resources or inject predictable entropy if the captured packets correspond to known random data, with the five-minute default window balancing security considerations limiting the temporal window for replay opportunity against operational robustness tolerating network delays and moderate clock drift between Collector and Gateway systems.

Sequence number verification provides additional replay protection through monotonically increasing integers embedded in each packet, with the Gateway maintaining the last successfully processed sequence number and rejecting any packet exhibiting a sequence number less than or equal to the last observed value, thereby detecting replay attempts even within the temporal validity window where timestamp validation alone would permit acceptance. Sequence number gaps indicating packet loss receive logging for operational monitoring purposes but do not trigger rejection, distinguishing legitimate network packet loss from malicious replay attempts and enabling continued operation despite imperfect network reliability. The combined verification protocol requires that incoming packets successfully pass all four independent checks including CRC32 checksum verification detecting transmission errors, HMAC signature verification authenticating the sender and detecting tampering, timestamp validation ensuring temporal freshness, and sequence number verification preventing replay attacks, with failure of any single check causing immediate packet rejection and detailed logging for security audit purposes.

### 2.4 Model Context Protocol Integration

The MCP server component implements a comprehensive bridge between quantum entropy infrastructure and artificial intelligence systems through adoption of the Model Context Protocol, an emerging standard for AI agent tool integration developed by Anthropic that establishes JSON-RPC 2.0 messaging patterns over multiple transport mechanisms including standard input/output streams for local AI assistants and HTTP connections for remote client integration [7]. This architectural choice positions quantum randomness as a first-class capability accessible to AI agents through the same standardized interfaces they employ for file system operations, web searches, and computational tools, thereby eliminating the substantial integration friction that previously required custom HTTP client implementations, authentication logic, binary data parsing routines, and error recovery mechanisms for each AI platform and QRNG service combination.

The implementation exposes quantum randomness through five carefully designed tools that address distinct use cases encountered in AI-assisted research workflows while maintaining consistent patterns for parameter specification and result formatting. The random bytes tool accepts length specifications ranging from one to one million forty-eight thousand five hundred seventy-six bytes with selectable encoding in hexadecimal or base64 formats, serving cryptographic key generation scenarios requiring specific binary lengths and encoding compatibility with diverse cryptographic libraries and frameworks. The random integers tool generates arrays of up to ten thousand quantum random integers within caller-specified minimum and maximum bounds through rejection sampling algorithms that eliminate modulo bias, supporting experimental parameter selection, statistical sampling, and stochastic simulation initialization. The random floats tool produces arrays of quantum random floating-point values uniformly distributed across the half-open interval from zero inclusive to one exclusive, addressing Monte Carlo integration, probability distribution sampling, and continuous stochastic processes requiring high-quality uniform variates. The random UUID tool generates version four universally unique identifiers incorporating quantum randomness in place of pseudo-random sources typically employed by standard libraries, providing enhanced uniqueness guarantees for experimental session tracking, database record identification, and distributed system coordination. The randomness validation tool performs on-demand statistical quality assessment through Monte Carlo π estimation across configurable iteration counts ranging from one thousand to one hundred million, returning numerical estimates, error analysis, and qualitative ratings that enable researchers to verify entropy source health and detect degradation without deploying external statistical test frameworks.

Each tool responds with structured JSON objects incorporating not only the requested random data but also source attribution explicitly identifying the randomness as quantum-derived rather than pseudo-random, enabling AI agents to maintain methodological transparency in research outputs through automatic citation of entropy sources in experimental documentation and scientific publications. The MCP server handles all communication complexity with the Gateway including authentication token management, transient network error recovery through exponential backoff retry strategies, connection pooling for efficiency across repeated requests, and graceful degradation when the Gateway becomes temporarily unavailable, presenting a simplified interface to AI agents that abstracts infrastructure concerns and enables zero-configuration quantum randomness access for researchers lacking expertise in distributed systems or network programming.### 2.5 Multi-Source Entropy Aggregation

QRNG-DD incorporates sophisticated mechanisms for combining entropy from multiple QRNG appliances simultaneously, addressing risks inherent in single-source dependencies including catastrophic hardware failures that completely eliminate entropy availability, theoretical concerns regarding potential manufacturer backdoors or undisclosed biases in proprietary quantum measurement apparatus, and vendor-specific statistical artifacts that might influence particular application domains. The system supports two mathematically distinct mixing strategies selected based on the correlation characteristics and trustworthiness assumptions of the constituent entropy sources, enabling deployment flexibility across diverse security requirements and risk tolerance profiles.

For scenarios involving genuinely independent quantum sources where each appliance operates on isolated quantum phenomena without shared physical infrastructure or synchronized timing mechanisms, exclusive-or combination provides provable information-theoretic security guarantees rooted in fundamental properties of Boolean algebra and probability theory [8], specifically that if at least one source generates uniformly distributed random bits independent of all other sources regardless of their quality or potential compromise, then the exclusive-or composition of all sources necessarily produces uniformly distributed output that inherits the security strength of the strongest constituent source while remaining robust against compromise of weaker sources.

In contexts involving potentially correlated sources arising from shared environmental factors, synchronized timing circuits, or common manufacturing processes, or when integrating quantum entropy with high-quality pseudo-random sources as defense-in-depth against quantum hardware failures, the HMAC-based Key Derivation Function provides superior statistical properties through cryptographic extraction and expansion operations [11] that transform the concatenated multi-source input into uniformly distributed output even when individual sources exhibit complex correlation patterns, thereby guaranteeing that the resulting entropy maintains cryptographic quality suitable for key generation and security-critical applications.

The Collector continuously monitors the health and reliability of each configured entropy source through independent tracking of success rates, failure patterns, response latencies, and data quality indicators, implementing automatic fault isolation whereby sources exceeding configurable failure thresholds such as three consecutive unsuccessful fetch attempts become temporarily excluded from the aggregation process while remaining under active monitoring for recovery. Automatic recovery mechanisms periodically retry failed sources at sixty-second intervals to detect restoration of service availability, ensuring the system dynamically adapts to transient network issues, appliance maintenance windows, and hardware degradation without requiring manual intervention or compromising entropy availability through dependency on unreliable sources.

### 4.2 Validation and Accuracy

Independent validation through multiple complementary methodologies confirms both the quantum entropy quality and the correctness of system implementation across security, performance, and statistical dimensions. Monte Carlo π estimation employing ten million random coordinate pair generations achieves a numerical estimate of 3.141598 representing an absolute error of 0.000005 and relative error of 0.0002 percent, demonstrating statistical quality matching high-quality pseudo-random generators while providing the fundamental unpredictability guarantee that only quantum sources can deliver, with this level of precision indicating no detectable bias in the entropy distribution that might suggest hardware malfunction, software bugs, or cryptographic transformation artifacts. Frequency distribution analysis across one million bytes applying chi-square goodness-of-fit tests confirms uniform distribution with test statistic 248.73 remaining well below the critical value 293.25 at significance level 0.05, formally supporting the null hypothesis that the observed distribution matches the expected uniform distribution and detecting no systematic bias toward particular byte values. Autocorrelation analysis examining correlation between entropy values at various offsets reveals maximum absolute correlation coefficient 0.0031 across all tested lags, confirming statistical independence between sequential entropy values and demonstrating absence of predictable patterns that would indicate pseudo-random augmentation or deterministic transformations.

Cryptographic integrity verification proceeds through comprehensive test suites implementing adversarial scenarios including deliberately tampered packets with modified entropy payloads to verify HMAC detection, replayed packets captured from legitimate traffic to verify sequence number and timestamp protection, packets with expired timestamps to verify freshness validation, and packets with artificial sequence gaps to verify resilience to network packet loss, with all malicious packets correctly rejected and logged with appropriate error codes while legitimate packets experiencing network delays or reordering receive proper handling. Constant-time HMAC comparison receives specific validation through timing analysis measuring verification duration across one hundred thousand comparisons with varying input characteristics, demonstrating standard deviation below one microsecond indicating no measurable timing variation correlated with input similarity that could enable timing side-channel attacks attempting to infer secret key bits through statistical analysis of verification latencies.

Performance validation through sustained load testing employing industry-standard benchmarking tools confirms stated performance characteristics under realistic operational conditions, with ten-minute continuous tests using ten concurrent clients generating 59,842 total requests representing sustained throughput of 99.7 requests per second with variance below ten percent indicating stable performance without gradual degradation. Extended twenty-four-hour continuous operation testing verifies system stability through metrics including zero unplanned process terminations indicating absence of crash-inducing bugs, 99.7 percent buffer read success rate demonstrating reliable entropy availability, and constant memory utilization indicating absence of memory leaks that would manifest as gradually increasing resident set size eventually culminating in out-of-memory failures.

Security assessment through independent review by external security researchers applying threat modeling methodologies confirms correct implementation of data diode principles, specifically validating through architectural analysis and source code inspection that the Gateway possesses no code paths capable of initiating connections toward the Collector even under adversarial scenarios including Gateway process compromise, configuration file modification, or malicious client requests attempting to exploit implementation vulnerabilities. ## 5. Reusability and Extensibility

### 5.1 Reusability

The architectural patterns embodied in QRNG-DD transcend the specific quantum entropy distribution application to address a broader class of problems requiring secure unidirectional information flow across network trust boundaries, with the fundamental design demonstrating applicability wherever organizations must extract data from protected networks for external processing while preventing reverse access that could compromise security or introduce contamination. Medical research institutions managing patient care networks subject to HIPAA privacy regulations and research networks conducting statistical analyses face similar requirements, with the split architecture enabling Collector-like components operating within clinical networks to extract anonymized patient records, treatment outcomes, and epidemiological data before pushing through cryptographically verified channels to Gateway-like components serving research databases and analysis platforms, all while preventing reverse data flow that could enable privacy breaches through patient record access or malware introduction into healthcare systems managing life-critical medical devices.

Industrial control systems governing manufacturing processes, power generation facilities, and chemical plants increasingly require telemetry extraction for predictive maintenance, efficiency optimization, and regulatory compliance reporting, yet direct network connections between operational technology networks managing physical processes and information technology networks providing business services create attack vectors enabling potential disruption of physical operations through cyber intrusions. Software data diode architectures adapted from QRNG-DD enable push-only telemetry transmission from OT to IT networks without creating bidirectional communication paths exploitable for unauthorized command injection, with cryptographic integrity verification ensuring that transmitted telemetry accurately reflects actual operational conditions rather than manipulated data designed to conceal equipment failures or malicious operational changes.

Financial institutions operating high-frequency trading systems on networks isolated from corporate infrastructure for performance and security reasons require transaction log extraction for compliance monitoring, risk management, and audit trail maintenance, with regulatory requirements demanding complete immutable records of all trading activity while security considerations prohibit bidirectional network connections that could enable unauthorized market manipulation or confidential strategy exfiltration. Adaptation of the software data diode pattern enables trading systems to push transaction logs to compliance systems through cryptographically authenticated channels with temporal validation ensuring log freshness and sequence verification preventing selective log omission attempts, providing comprehensive audit capabilities while maintaining trading system isolation from corporate networks and Internet threats.

The reusable components embodied in the shared core library provide value beyond integrated system deployment, with cryptographic utilities implementing HMAC signature generation and verification, CRC32 checksum computation, and constant-time comparison algorithms serving as building blocks for other Rust projects requiring similar security primitives, while the circular buffer implementations optimized for concurrent access demonstrate patterns applicable to high-performance data streaming applications, message queuing systems, and real-time data processing pipelines. The Model Context Protocol server implementation provides reference architecture for developers creating MCP tool servers exposing other specialized capabilities to AI agents, demonstrating JSON-RPC protocol handling, parameter validation, error formatting, and dual transport support through patterns directly applicable to exposing computational tools, domain-specific databases, laboratory instruments, or simulation systems to AI-assisted research workflows.

QRNG-DD incorporates deliberate extension mechanisms enabling customization and enhancement without modifying core system code through well-defined interfaces and plugin architectures that preserve upgrade compatibility while supporting organization-specific requirements. Custom entropy source integration proceeds through implementation of trait interfaces defining the contract for entropy providers, requiring only a fetch method accepting desired byte length and returning result types encoding success with entropy payload or failure with descriptive error information, thereby enabling support for QRNG appliances beyond ID Quantique's Quantis family including Whitewood's netRandom, PicoQuant's quantum random number generators, or even high-quality pseudo-random sources used for testing and development purposes, with plugin registration through declarative configuration enabling mixed deployments incorporating multiple heterogeneous source types within single Collector instances.

Organizations implementing proprietary quality assessment algorithms might weight entropy contributions based on real-time source health metrics derived from continuous statistical testing, while regulatory frameworks mandating minimum numbers of independent entropy sources could implement threshold-based mixing refusing to produce output unless specified minimum source counts contribute successfully, ensuring compliance with security standards requiring diversity through implementation-enforced policies rather than operational procedures subject to human error.

Statistical quality testing extends beyond the built-in Monte Carlo π estimation through integration of comprehensive test suites including NIST SP 800-22 providing fifteen distinct statistical tests examining frequency distributions, longest runs, spectral characteristics, and other randomness properties [12], Dieharder implementing numerous tests developed by the cryptographic research community [13], and domain-specific tests addressing application requirements such as avalanche criterion evaluation for cryptographic key generation or correlation analysis for Monte Carlo simulation initialization. ## 6. Limitations and Future Development

### 6.1 Current Limitations

Software-based data diode implementation provides substantially weaker isolation guarantees compared to hardware solutions employing physical transmission mechanisms with removed receive capability, relying instead on correct firewall configuration, operating system security, and application architecture to enforce unidirectional flow properties that remain vulnerable to configuration errors introducing inadvertent reverse communication paths, operating system vulnerabilities enabling arbitrary code execution with network access, and insider threats with system administrator privileges capable of modifying firewall rules or application code. Organizations deploying QRNG-DD must maintain rigorous configuration management practices including version-controlled firewall rules subject to peer review, regular security audits validating actual network topology against intended architecture, deployment validation scripts automatically verifying that firewall rules permit only intended traffic flows, and operating system hardening following security frameworks such as CIS benchmarks or DISA STIGs, recognizing that software isolation ultimately depends on the cumulative security of multiple layers rather than single physical guarantee characterizing hardware alternatives. Critical infrastructure applications including nuclear facility monitoring, military command and control systems, and financial market surveillance should continue employing hardware data diodes despite higher costs given the catastrophic consequences potential compromise could enable, while QRNG-DD addresses research environments, corporate laboratories, and moderate-security production systems where multi-layer software security provides adequate protection given realistic threat models and acceptable risk tolerance.

The temporal replay protection window currently configured at five minutes represents a security-usability tradeoff wherein shorter windows provide stronger replay attack protection by limiting the time during which captured packets remain valid for retransmission but introduce operational fragility through increased sensitivity to network delays and clock drift, while longer windows improve operational robustness at the cost of extended replay vulnerability periods. Although sequence number verification mitigates replay risks within the temporal window by rejecting duplicate sequence numbers regardless of timestamp validity, the initial five-minute period following first packet receipt remains theoretically vulnerable to replay attacks, with the current configuration representing balanced compromise based on empirical testing showing that network delays rarely exceed thirty seconds in typical deployment environments while five-minute windows accommodate moderate clock drift and temporary network congestion without triggering false rejections. Organizations operating in environments with particularly unreliable networks might increase the window to fifteen or thirty minutes accepting increased replay risk, while high-security environments with tightly controlled network characteristics could reduce the window to thirty or sixty seconds trading operational resilience for enhanced security.

The current Collector implementation serializes push operations to the Gateway through single-threaded transmission loops processing buffer contents sequentially, imposing a theoretical maximum push frequency of approximately twelve pushes per minute given five-second intervals between transmissions and preventing concurrent transmission of multiple entropy packets when network bandwidth substantially exceeds entropy generation rate. This architectural choice reflects the current design focus on reliability and simplicity rather than maximum throughput, proving adequate for deployments where buffer capacities and fetch rates balance client request rates without requiring continuous high-speed transmission, yet limiting scalability for scenarios involving dozens of concurrent QRNG appliances generating multiple megabytes per second of quantum entropy that exceeds serial transmission capacity. Future enhancement enabling pipelined concurrent pushes could reduce transmission latency through parallel network operations while maintaining packet ordering through sequence number coordination, though implementation complexity increases substantially through additional synchronization requirements and error handling for partial transmission failures.

The absence of persistent entropy storage represents a deliberate architectural choice prioritizing real-time distribution over archival requirements, with the volatile Gateway buffer contents disappearing upon process restart and eliminating any historical record of distributed entropy beyond application-level logging of request metadata. While this design proves appropriate for typical use cases where clients consume entropy immediately for cryptographic operations or simulations, research applications requiring reproducible experiments through ability to retrieve identical entropy sequences, security audits demanding complete records of all distributed randomness for forensic analysis, or regulatory compliance mandating archival of cryptographic key material generation evidence would benefit from optional persistent storage capabilities. The planned Entropy Archive feature would record entropy packets to tamper-evident append-only storage with cryptographic chaining linking sequential records, though implementation remains future work given current focus on core distribution functionality and the substantial complexity persistent storage introduces through durability requirements, backup procedures, and retention policy enforcement.

### 6.2 Planned Enhancements

Large language model integration could substantially reduce deployment complexity through automated analysis of network topology descriptions provided in natural language, generation of recommended firewall rule configurations tailored to specific organizational requirements and security policies, and creation of complete deployment packages including Docker Compose orchestration files, environment variable configurations, and monitoring integration specifications. Researchers providing simple textual descriptions of their network architecture including internal network addressing, external network configuration, QRNG appliance locations, and security requirements would receive generated configuration artifacts ready for deployment following validation review, reducing deployment time from hours of manual configuration file editing and rule crafting to minutes of natural language interaction with AI systems possessing knowledge of QRNG-DD architecture and security best practices.

Enhanced quality monitoring through integration of comprehensive statistical test suites would provide deeper insight into quantum entropy characteristics and enable automated detection of subtle degradation potentially indicating hardware failures or environmental interference before such degradation produces catastrophic entropy quality loss. Integration of NIST SP 800-22 statistical test suite comprising fifteen distinct tests examining frequency characteristics, spectral properties, complexity measures, and other randomness indicators would enable periodic background execution generating comprehensive quality reports documenting the statistical health of quantum sources [12], while comparative benchmarking automatically generating equivalent quantities of pseudo-random data for parallel statistical testing would enable anomaly detection through identification of cases where quantum sources produce statistical characteristics resembling pseudo-random generators potentially indicating hardware malfunction, environmental interference, or configuration errors causing fallback to deterministic algorithms.

HTTP/2 protocol support would improve efficiency for client access patterns involving high-frequency small requests through multiplexing enabling multiple concurrent requests over single TCP connections, server push proactively transmitting anticipated data before explicit requests, and header compression reducing overhead for requests sharing common authentication credentials and parameters. Estimated latency reductions of twenty to thirty percent for typical workloads primarily benefit interactive applications where human users perceive response time differences, while bulk entropy retrieval for large-scale simulations would see minimal improvement given that transmission time dominates over protocol overhead for megabyte-sized transfers.

Kubernetes operator implementation would enable cloud-native deployment with automated lifecycle management including initial deployment of Collector and Gateway components with proper network policy configuration, horizontal scaling of Gateway instances based on request load metrics automatically provisioning additional pods when throughput demands exceed single-instance capacity, automatic secret rotation for HMAC keys and API tokens following security best practices limiting cryptographic key lifetimes, and self-healing through automatic restart of failed components with exponential backoff preventing rapid failure loops. Single-command deployment to managed Kubernetes services including Google Kubernetes Engine, Amazon Elastic Kubernetes Service, and Azure Kubernetes Service would make QRNG-DD accessible to organizations preferring cloud infrastructure over on-premise deployment, though careful attention to network isolation remains essential when deploying the Collector component given its privileged access to QRNG appliances.

Additional Model Context Protocol transport implementations would expand AI agent accessibility beyond the current standard input/output transport serving local AI assistants, with HTTP transport enabling remote MCP clients to access quantum randomness over network connections supporting distributed AI systems executing on separate infrastructure from the QRNG deployment, and WebSocket support providing persistent bidirectional connections enabling efficient real-time randomness streaming for AI agents conducting long-running simulations or experiments requiring continuous entropy supply without connection re-establishment overhead characterizing stateless HTTP request-response patterns.

### 6.3 Research Directions

Historical data collection regarding entropy consumption patterns across research projects and application domains could enable investigation of correlations between quantum randomness usage and research outcomes, addressing fundamental questions about whether projects employing quantum entropy in cryptographic protocol development, Monte Carlo simulations, or quantum algorithm validation produce measurably different result quality compared to equivalent projects using pseudo-random sources. Analysis of sufficient experimental sample sizes through retrospective studies or prospective randomized trials where researchers receive either quantum or pseudo-random entropy without knowledge of the assignment could provide evidence-based guidance about which research domains genuinely benefit from true randomness versus those where high-quality pseudo-random generators prove statistically indistinguishable, informing resource allocation decisions for organizations considering QRNG hardware investments.

Investigation of AI-quantum interaction patterns through detailed logging of MCP tool invocations by artificial intelligence agents could reveal how AI systems reason about and consume randomness, addressing questions including whether agents request appropriate quantities for stated tasks or systematically over- or under-request entropy, whether agents demonstrate understanding of tradeoffs between quantum entropy providing true unpredictability at higher latency and computational cost versus pseudo-random generation offering higher performance at weaker security guarantees, and how agent behavior evolves through learning from feedback about randomness quality assessment results. Such research could inform design of more effective AI-research tool interfaces through identification of common usage patterns, typical error modes, and successful interaction strategies, while potentially revealing fundamental insights about AI reasoning capabilities regarding probabilistic concepts and uncertainty quantification.

Formal verification of software data diode security properties under various failure modes and attack scenarios would provide rigorous mathematical characterization of achievable guarantees beyond informal architectural analysis and manual code review, employing techniques from formal methods including model checking exhaustively exploring system state spaces to identify reachable states violating security properties, theorem proving mechanically verifying that implementation code satisfies unidirectional flow specifications, and information flow analysis statically proving that no code paths enable reverse data transmission from Gateway to Collector. Quantitative comparison of software data diode guarantees against hardware alternatives through adversarial capability modeling would support risk-based deployment decisions by clearly characterizing under which threat models software isolation proves adequate versus scenarios requiring physical transmission guarantees, providing security practitioners with evidence-based guidance for technology selection rather than relying on general principles or vendor claims.

Multi-source mixing optimization research could investigate optimal entropy combination strategies for sources exhibiting various correlation characteristics, addressing questions about whether machine learning techniques can automatically detect source correlations from output statistics without a priori knowledge of physical processes or manufacturing relationships, how mixing strategy selection should adapt based on observed correlation levels through automated switching between exclusive-or for independent sources and HMAC-based key derivation for correlated sources, and whether advanced mixing algorithms beyond simple exclusive-or and HKDF can extract additional security or statistical quality from partially correlated sources. Automated correlation detection and adaptive mixing strategy selection would eliminate manual configuration requirements while ensuring optimal entropy quality regardless of source characteristics, potentially enabling opportunistic incorporation of lower-trust entropy sources that enhance security when properly mixed despite insufficient quality for standalone use.

## 7. Conclusions

QRNG-DD demonstrates that software-based data diode architecture implementing unidirectional data flow through architectural constraints and cryptographic integrity verification rather than physical transmission limitations provides practical quantum entropy distribution with performance characteristics, security properties, and cost profiles suitable for academic research institutions, corporate research laboratories, and moderate-security production deployments that do not involve critical infrastructure protection or classified information processing. The system achieves sustained throughput approaching one hundred requests per second with median latency below ten milliseconds through systematic application of modern concurrent programming patterns including zero-copy buffer operations, lock-free synchronization optimized for read-heavy workloads, and asynchronous input/output processing, delivering performance improvements ranging from six to twenty times faster than public QRNG services while reducing total five-year costs by ninety-eight to ninety-nine percent compared to commercial hardware data diode solutions through elimination of capital equipment acquisition while maintaining security properties adequate for applications where multi-layer software protection backed by rigorous cryptographic integrity verification suffices.

The integration with the Model Context Protocol represents the first implementation of quantum randomness access specifically designed for artificial intelligence agent consumption through standardized tool interfaces employing JSON-RPC messaging patterns, positioning quantum entropy as a first-class capability accessible through identical interaction patterns that AI systems employ for file operations, computational tools, and web services, thereby eliminating substantial integration friction that previously required custom HTTP client development, authentication implementation, binary data parsing, and error recovery logic consuming multiple hours of development effort per AI platform and QRNG service combination. Built-in statistical validation through Monte Carlo π estimation achieving error rates below two ten-thousandths of one percent with ten million iterations, multi-source entropy aggregation implementing mathematically sound mixing strategies including exclusive-or for independent sources and HMAC-based key derivation for potentially correlated sources, and comprehensive production observability through Prometheus metrics and structured JSON logging provide capabilities typically absent from academic prototypes and research services, enabling confident deployment in research infrastructure requiring monitoring, alerting, capacity planning, and incident response capabilities.

As artificial intelligence systems increasingly support scientific research workflows through experimental parameter selection, data analysis automation, and iterative hypothesis refinement, and as quantum computing research expands demanding classical computational infrastructure for test vector generation, output validation, and algorithm development, standardized access to high-quality quantum randomness emerges as critical research infrastructure enabling reproducible methodologies, transparent provenance documentation, and elimination of systematic biases that pseudo-random generators could introduce. QRNG-DD provides this infrastructure through open-source software with MIT licensing enabling independent security audits by academic researchers and security practitioners, reproducible research through transparent implementation accessible for inspection and validation, and community-driven improvements through collaborative development addressing emerging requirements and discovered limitations, ensuring that quantum entropy remains accessible to researchers worldwide rather than concentration in well-funded institutions capable of acquiring expensive commercial solutions.

The architectural patterns demonstrated including split component design separating trusted and untrusted networks, push-only communication initiated exclusively from protected sides, cryptographic integrity verification preventing tampering and authenticating sources, and temporal validity checking ensuring data freshness generalize beyond quantum entropy distribution to encompass diverse scenarios requiring unidirectional information flow across trust boundaries, including medical data extraction from clinical to research networks, industrial telemetry transmission from operational to analytical infrastructure, and financial transaction logging from trading to compliance systems. Future development focusing on large language model-assisted deployment reducing configuration complexity, enhanced statistical quality monitoring through comprehensive test suite integration, and formal security verification providing rigorous mathematical characterization of achievable guarantees will strengthen both the platform's research utility and its applicability to production environments with stringent reliability and assurance requirements.

We invite contributions from quantum computing researchers investigating fundamental properties of quantum randomness, cryptography practitioners developing security protocols requiring high-quality entropy, AI researchers exploring integration patterns between artificial intelligence and quantum resources, and software architects addressing general unidirectional data flow requirements to collaborate in expanding capabilities, validating security properties, implementing additional entropy sources and mixing strategies, and applying QRNG-DD to novel use cases that advance scientific knowledge while demonstrating practical value. The open-source implementation ensures that quantum randomness remains democratized rather than proprietary, accessible rather than restricted, and transparent rather than opaque, thereby accelerating scientific discovery through reduced barriers to essential research infrastructure.
